1996

On October 2, 1996, Lance Armstrong was diagnosed withtesticular cancer that had already metastasized malignantly, spreadingto his lungs and brain. In later interviews, his doctor revealed that hischances of survival at the time were "almost non-existent." They toldLance he had a 20% to 50% chance, mainly to give him hope. But withhis type of cancer, X-ray tests and blood work showed very little hope.In commemoration of this day, Nike later launched the "10//2"merchandise series, with a portion of the proceeds going to the LanceArmstrong Foundation established in 1997. After undergoing surgeryto remove his right testicle and the affected part of his brain,Armstrong underwent a series of chemotherapy treatments at IndianaUniversity Medical Center.


In 1998, Lance Armstrong's cycling comeback faced initial challenges.as he struggled to compete at an elite level in races like Paris-Nice,prompting thoughts of retirement. However, he relocated to NorthCarolina to train with former Tour de France rider Bob Roll and others,finding renewed determination. During this time, he organized charityevents for cancer research, including the Race for the Roses in Austin,Texas, where notable cyclists like Greg LeMond, Sean Kelly, and MiguelIndurain attended. Armstrong expressed admiration for LaurentJalabert during this period.


1999

Armstrong puts on the Maillot jaune -- theyellow jersey after winning the prologue ofthe 1999 Tour de France.

Armstrong's resurgence gained momentum in 1998 when he won the Tour of Luxembourg and notably performed well in the Vuelta aEspana, securing a top-five finish in individual time trials and stayingcompetitive with the general classification contenders in themountains, finishing 4th overall. His success earned him recognitionand he finished 3rd in the voting for the Velo d'Or.


In 1999, Armstrong achieved his breakthrough by winning the Tour deFrance, with significant stage victories. He dominated the race,beating his closest rival, Alex Zulle by a substantial margin. Theabsence of Jan Ullrich and Marco Pantani during this tour meantArmstrong had yet to prove himself against some of the sport'sbiggest names.


2000

Overall leader Lance Armstrong, left in yellow.and the rest of the pack climb Tamie passduring the ninth stage of the 1999 Tour deFrance. Armstrong won the stage, andeventually the race.

The year 2000 marked the return of ullrich and Pantani sparking a competitive rivalry with Armstrong. Armstrong triumphed, winningthe Tour by 6 mins and 2 seconds over Ullrich. He claimed a stagevictory in the individual time trial.


ln 2001, Armstrong continued his dominance, beating Ullrich by 6minutes and 44 seconds. The following year, Ullrich was absent due tosuspension, allowing Armstrong to secure victory by a seven-minutemargin over Joseba Beloki. During key stages, Armstrong's US Postalteam, led by Vuelta champion Roberto Heras, played a crucial role insecuring his wins, particularly on challenging climbs.


ln 2003, Lance Armstrong continued his dominant streak by winninghis fifth consecutive Tour de France, with Jan Ullrich finishing secondTheir battle was incredibly close, with only a minute and a secondseparating them in Paris. Armstrong's U.S. Postal team won the teamtime trial, and he nearly crashed out of the Tour while defending theyellow jersey on stage 9. During a treacherous descent, Armstrongnarrowly avoided a crash when Joseba Beloki fell violently. In doingso, Armstrong went off the road onto a foot trail but managed tokeep upright, picking up his bike and continuing without losing time.Armstrong maintained a slim 21-second lead over AlexanderVinokourov, while Ullrich emerged as his main challenger. Armstrongsecured stage 15 after an incident when a spectator's bag caught hishandlebar.

In 2004, Armstrong's supremacy continued as he clinched his sixthconsecutive Tour de France victory. He finished 6 minutes and 19seconds ahead of Andreas Klden, with Ullrich coming in fourth.Armstrong won a record-breaking five individual stages and the teamtime trial. He also achieved a remarkable feat by winning threeconsecutive mountain stages and excelled in individual time trials. Hisvictory on the famous Alpe d'Huez climb showcased his exceptionalriding skills as he overtook Basso. Armstrong also won sprint finishesand the final individual time trial, completing a record-breakingnumber of stage wins.


In 2005, Lance Armstrong faced strong competition, narrowly losingthe stage 1 time trial to David Zabriskie by just two seconds, despitepassing Jan Ullrich. His Discovery Channel team excelled in the teamtime trial, and Armstrong clinched victory in the final individual timetrial. Throughout the mountain stages, he faced challenges from lvanBasso, Jan Ullrich, Andreas Kloden, Alexandre Vinokourov, and formerteammate Levi Leipheimer. Armstrong skillfully defended his lead andat times, even extended it. On July 24, he made history by winning hisseventh consecutive Tour de France, finishing 4 minutes and 40seconds ahead of Basso, with Ullrich in third place.


In the same year, Armstrong announced his retirement fromprofessional cycling, citing a desire to spend more time with his familyand his foundation. During his retirement, he briefly rekindled hisconnection to cycling when he witnessed Carlos Sastre's victory onAlpe d'Huez at a conference in 2008.
